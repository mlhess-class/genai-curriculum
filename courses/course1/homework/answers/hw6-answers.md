# HW6 Answer Key: Personal AI Toolkit Design

**For TA Use Only**

---

## Part 1: Needs Assessment (10 points)

**Check for:**
- 8-10 tasks listed (not fewer)
- Tasks are specific to the student's actual field/major (not generic "writing emails")
- AI potential ratings are justified (High/Medium/Low with reasoning)
- Time estimates are realistic

**Strong example (Nursing student):**
| Task | Time | AI Potential | Justification |
|------|------|-------------|---------------|
| Writing patient care summaries | 45 min/day | High | AI can draft from structured notes |
| Studying pharmacology flashcards | 2 hr/week | High | AI can generate practice questions |
| Researching evidence-based practices | 3 hr/week | Medium | AI can summarize but needs verification for medical accuracy |
| Shift scheduling coordination | 30 min/week | Low | Requires institutional system access |

**Common mistakes:**
- Tasks that are too vague ("doing homework," "being productive")
- Rating everything as "High" without considering limitations
- Tasks unrelated to their actual context (copying generic lists)

## Part 2: Tool Selection (15 points, 3 per tool)

**For each of the 5 tools, verify:**
- Tool is real and appropriate for the task (1 pt)
- Comparison to alternatives is specific, not generic (1 pt) — bonus if referencing HW1 experience
- Cost and privacy considerations are accurate and researched (1 pt)

**Common mistakes:**
- Listing tools they've never actually used
- Privacy analysis that's just "it collects data" without specifics (what data? is it used for training? can you opt out?)
- Not mentioning free tier limitations (e.g., ChatGPT free vs Plus differences)

**Strong answers** connect to earlier assignments: "In HW1 I found Claude better for long-form writing, so I chose it for my report drafting workflow. In HW5 I found it hallucinated 3/10 times on citations, so I always verify references manually."

## Part 3: Workflow Design (15 points, 5 per workflow)

**Each workflow must have all 5 elements:**
1. Trigger ✓
2. Steps with specific tools/prompts ✓
3. Quality check ✓
4. Ethical guardrails ✓
5. Fallback plan ✓

**At least one visual diagram required.**

**Model workflow example:**

**Workflow: Weekly Research Summary**
1. *Trigger:* Monday morning, new articles saved to reading list
2. *Steps:* (a) Paste article abstracts into Claude with prompt: "Summarize each article in 2 sentences, noting methodology and key findings." (b) Review summaries against originals. (c) Ask AI to identify common themes across articles. (d) Draft 500-word synthesis.
3. *Quality check:* Verify all claims against original sources. Check for hallucinated connections.
4. *Guardrails:* Note in document: "Initial summaries AI-assisted; synthesis is original analysis." Don't input patient data.
5. *Fallback:* Manual summarization; takes 3x longer but works offline.

**Grading:**
- 13-15: All 3 workflows complete with all elements, diagram included, prompts are specific
- 10-12: Workflows present but 1-2 missing elements, or prompts are vague
- 7-9: 2 workflows or significant gaps
- <7: 1 workflow or incomplete

## Part 4: Implementation Test (5 points)

- Student must have actually done the thing, not hypothesized about it
- Honest assessment matters more than success — students who describe failures and revisions should score higher than those claiming perfection
- **Common mistake:** Describing what they "would do" in future tense instead of what they did

## Part 5: Presentation Outline (5 points)

- 5-minute talk should be structured (not just a list of tools)
- Should include some kind of demonstration or concrete example
- Time allocation noted
- **Full credit** if outline is specific enough that you can imagine the presentation

## General TA Notes

- This is the capstone homework. Quality should reflect growth across the semester.
- Students who reference specific earlier assignments (HW1 comparison, HW5 hallucination findings) show integration of learning — note this positively.
- The presentation outline feeds directly into the Week 15 final presentation. Flag students whose outlines are too vague to present.
- Watch for toolkits that are just "use ChatGPT for everything." The point is thoughtful, diverse tool selection with justified choices.
